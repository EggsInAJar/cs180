Project 1: Perspective Distortion Analysis

Understanding Focal Length Effects on Facial Proportions

Project Overview

This project demonstrates how different camera focal lengths and subject distances affect the perceived proportions of facial features, particularly the nose and ears. The analysis shows the relationship between perspective compression and natural-looking proportions.

Part 1: Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

85mm @ 200cm

Most natural proportions, ears visible, background compressed

35mm @ 85cm

Slight nose enlargement, ears less prominent

16mm @ 40cm

Noticeable nose projection, ears receding

12mm @ 30cm

Pronounced distortion, ears barely visible

8mm @ 20cm

Maximum distortion, fish-eye effect

Understanding the Effect

When you take a photo up close to the subject (Erfan Ballew), the camera exaggerates perspective. This means features closer to the lens (like the nose) look much larger than those farther away (like the ears), creating distortion. When you step back and zoom in, the relative distance between facial features becomes small compared to the camera’s distance, so the proportions look more natural. Zooming then fills the frame without reintroducing distortion. That’s why the second portrait looks more realistic and flattering.

Understanding the Effect

When you zoom in from far away, the camera compresses distances along the depth of the scene. Objects that are actually far apart (like buildings down a street) look closer together and makes the scene look “flattened.” When you move closer and use a wider angle, nearby objects look much larger relative to distant ones, so the depth of the street feels stretched out.
